✅ Safe Payment Methods

  • Check payable to a business name — provides a paper trail and the name should match the event organizer's registered business.
  • PayPal (Goods & Services) — NOT "Friends & Family." Goods & Services has buyer protection. If they insist on Friends & Family, walk away.
  • Eventbrite, Zapplication, or Juried Art Services — established platforms designed for event registration with built-in protection.
  • Credit card — your bank can do a chargeback if the event is fraudulent. Always the safest option for large fees.
  • Square / Stripe invoices — legitimate businesses use these. Check that the invoice email domain matches the event's official domain.

🚫 Payment Methods to Refuse

  • Zelle — no buyer protection whatsoever. Money is gone the instant you send it.
  • Venmo (Friends & Family) — same as above. No recourse.
  • Cash App — peer-to-peer with no dispute mechanism for this type of fraud.
  • Wire transfer / bank transfer — irreversible and untraceable once sent.
  • Gift cards — if anyone ever asks you to pay a booth fee with Google Play or iTunes gift cards, it is 100% a scam. Always.
  • Cryptocurrency — no recourse if fraudulent.

⚠️ Before You Pay Anything

  • Call the event organizer on a phone number listed on their official website — not a number from the email they sent you.
  • Search "[Event Name] scam" or "[Event Name] vendor reviews" before paying.
  • Ask in Facebook crafter groups — communities like "Craft Fair Vendors" or local state groups often know which events are legit.
  • Verify the organizer's business is registered in their state (most states have a free online business search tool).
  • Screenshot everything — the invitation, the payment request, and the organizer's contact info.

🔍 How to Verify an Email Is Really From the Event

Scammers impersonate real events. Here's how to check:

  • Hover over the sender name to see the actual email address (don't just read the display name).
  • The domain after the @ should match their official website exactly. @renegadecraft.com is real; @renegadecraft-events.net may not be.
  • Go to the event's official website yourself and look for a login portal or vendor contact — don't click links from the email.
  • If in doubt, reply to the email but also CC the address listed on the official website, and see which reply comes back consistently.

📞 If You Think You've Been Scammed

  • Contact your bank immediately — the sooner you report it, the better your chance of recovery.
  • File a report at the FTC — reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • File an IC3 complaint — ic3.gov (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center)
  • Report to your state Attorney General — most have consumer fraud divisions.
  • Warn the crafting community — post in Facebook crafter groups so others aren't hit.

🚨 Report a Scam or Suspicious Listing

If you've encountered a fake event, a scam booth fee request, or someone impersonating a real event organizer, please report it. Your report helps protect the entire crafting community.

File a report through any or all of these channels — the more reports, the more likely action will be taken:

  • FTC (Federal Trade Commission) — reportfraud.ftc.gov — the primary US fraud reporting agency
  • FBI IC3 — ic3.gov — for internet-based fraud, especially if you lost money
  • Your state Attorney General — search "[your state] AG consumer fraud"
  • The platform where you found it — report the fake post/account to Facebook, Instagram, or Etsy directly
